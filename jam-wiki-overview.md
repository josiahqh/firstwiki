# Jam Wiki — Project Overview

## About the Group

A work-based music jam group with two core purposes:

1. **Bond over playing music** — a social and creative outlet for colleagues
2. **Improve musical literacy** — every session and song selection is an opportunity to explore and understand music more deeply

The group has approximately five members. Sessions involve live mixing of mic, MIDI, USB, acoustic, and plugged-in instruments, with tracking/recording gear present but not always the primary focus.

---

## What the Wiki Is For

The wiki serves as the group's shared memory and planning tool. It captures what gear is in use and how it is set up, what songs the group is working on and why, what happened in each session, and what the group wants to improve over time. It is intended to be maintained collaboratively by all five members.

---

## Site Structure

The wiki is organised into six top-level sections:

**Repertoire** — the heart of the wiki. Contains all song pages, the proposed songs inbox, and the planning subsection. Every song page is selected in part to illuminate a specific musical concept, tying the repertoire directly to the group's literacy goals.

**Sessions** — a reverse-chronological log of every jam session. Each entry records who attended, what was played, what worked, what needs more work, and what the group plans to do next.

**Members** — a roster of all five members, with each person's instruments, skill areas, gear contributions, and personal musical goals.

**Concepts** — a reference library of musical ideas the group is exploring. Each concept has its own page explaining the idea, listing songs in the repertoire that feature it, and suggesting exercises and listening recommendations.

**Reference** — technical setup documentation and general reference material. Covers how to get sound happening from scratch, signal flow, room setup, DAW templates, known problems and fixes, jam frameworks, and the group schedule.

**Home** — a welcome page with the group's mission statement and quick links to the most frequently used pages.

## Song Page Template

Each song in the repertoire follows a consistent template so that all entries are
easy to read, compare, and update. Every song page captures the same categories of
information, making the repertoire index easy to scan and keeping part assignments
and session notes in a predictable place.

### What Each Song Page Records

- **Title, artist, who proposed it, and when** — basic provenance for every entry
- **Status** — where the song is in the group's pipeline: Backlog, Actively Learning, Performance Ready, or Shelved
- **Key, tempo, and time signature**
- **Genre and difficulty**
- **Musical focus** — the primary (and optionally secondary) concept this song is meant to illuminate
- **Target practice date** — when the group plans to work on it next

### Page Sections

1. **Overview** — description of the song, why the group picked it, and a link to a reference recording
2. **Musical Focus** — the primary and secondary concept this song illuminates, what the group is specifically exploring, what to listen for, and further listening suggestions
3. **Key, Tempo & Structure** — song structure table (sections, bar counts, notes) and chord chart
4. **Part Assignments** — table of who plays what, plus per-member focus notes tailored to each person's instrument and skill goals
5. **Session Log** — running notes from each time the group practiced this song: what went well, what needs work, decisions made, next steps
6. **Arrangement Notes** — how the song has been adapted for the group's specific lineup
7. **Resources** — links to reference recording, chord chart, tab or sheet music, backing track, and tutorial videos

## Musical Concept System

Each song is selected in part to illuminate a specific musical concept. This gives
song selection a clear pedagogical purpose beyond personal preference, and ensures
the group's repertoire covers a healthy variety of musical ideas over time.

Every song carries a primary concept and optionally a secondary concept. These link
back to dedicated concept pages, which explain the idea in depth and list every song
in the repertoire that covers it. This bidirectional relationship means the group
can approach the repertoire from either direction: pick a song and discover its
concept, or pick a concept and find songs that teach it.

Planning benefits from this system too — the concept index makes it easy to spot
which areas (rhythm, harmony, dynamics, form, etc.) have been well covered and
which haven't been touched in a while.

### Concept Categories

| Category | What it covers |
|----------|---------------|
| Rhythm | Groove, syncopation, odd time signatures, pocket playing |
| Harmony | Chord progressions, modal harmony, voice leading |
| Melody | Improvisation, phrasing, motif development |
| Dynamics | Contrast, restraint, building and releasing tension |
| Form | Song structure, call and response, arrangement |
| Texture | Density, layering, space and silence |

### Example Concepts

## Contribution Model

All five members contribute to the wiki. The wiki is designed so that no contributor
needs to understand the underlying technical implementation — tools are provided that
simplify common tasks (adding a song, updating a song's status, recording a session)
into straightforward forms. Changes are reviewed by a maintainer before going live.

Session notes are captured collaboratively, allowing any member to comment before,
during, or after a session without editing any files directly. Known technical
problems are tracked the same way, keeping the troubleshooting page always current
without manual maintenance.
